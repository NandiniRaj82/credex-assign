# GTM.md — StackLens Go-To-Market Plan

## Exact Target User

Not "startups." The specific target is:

**Engineering Manager or CTO at a B2B SaaS company, Series A–B, 8–25 employees, based in the US or UK.**

Why this precision matters:
- They have enough employees that AI tool spend is noticeable ($500–5,000/month) but not enough that a dedicated procurement team is handling it
- They make or strongly influence purchasing decisions — no need to convince two layers of management
- They feel the pain personally: they approved the Cursor Business licenses, they're watching the AWS bill grow, and they have a vague sense they're not getting full utilisation
- They can action a recommendation the same day they see it — no RFP process, no legal review for a <$500/month tool swap

**The moment of intent:** They're reviewing monthly SaaS spend in Stripe, Ramp, or Brex — usually the first week of the month. They see the AI line item, sigh, and pay it. That's the moment StackLens needs to intercept.

## First 100 Users — 30 Days, $0 Budget

**Week 1 — Show HN + Reddit:**
Write a Show HN post titled: *"Show HN: StackLens – free AI spend auditor for startups (found $2.4k/yr avg)"*. The hook is the specific number. HN respects tools that do one thing well and show real data.

Post to `/r/ExperiencedDevs`: *"Built a tool to audit AI tool spend after our team of 6 was paying for 12 Copilot Enterprise seats. Saves the average user $2.4k/year. Free, no login."* Lead with the problem, not the product.

**Week 2 — Twitter/X thread:**
Write a data-driven thread: *"We audited 50 startup AI stacks. Here's what we found..."* — with specific, anonymised findings from StackLens results. Each point is a discovery that feels like inside information. End with the tool link. Tag engineers you see talking about AI costs.

**Week 3 — Direct outreach via Ramp/Brex communities:**
In the Ramp Slack `#saas-spend` channel: *"Anyone else auditing AI tool costs? We built something for this."* Not a product pitch — a genuine peer question that happens to have a natural follow-up.

**Week 4 — Embed in the wild:**
Reach out to 5 newsletters aimed at engineering managers (Pragmatic Engineer, The Developing Dev, Software Lead Weekly) and offer to write a guest piece: *"How to audit your team's AI tool spend in 10 minutes."* Naturally link StackLens.

## The Unfair Distribution Channel

Credex's existing customer base. Every company that has already bought AI credits through Credex is a potential StackLens user — and a referral case study. A Credex customer who used StackLens to audit their spend and then bought credits through Credex is a closed loop: the tool justifies the purchase and the purchase validates the tool.

No other competitor can walk into a Credex customer conversation and say *"here's a free tool that surfaces your overspend — and we're the company that can fix it."* That's a distribution moat.

## Week 1 Traction Targets (if this works)

- 150–300 audit completions from HN + Reddit (HN typically drives 500–2k unique visitors in 48 hours for a Show HN post that hits the front page)
- 15–30 email captures (10% conversion from audit completion is realistic)
- 3–5 Credex consultation bookings (from high-value cases >$500/mo savings)
- 1 organic repost from an engineering influencer account (>5k followers)

**What "it's working" looks like:** The shareable URL feature creates a second-order effect. If even 5% of users share their audit on Twitter, the OG card showing "$1,340/mo in savings found" is a more compelling ad than anything Credex could buy.
